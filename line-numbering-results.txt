[Line 24] Synaptic Transmission
[Line 25] * Definition: transmission of impulse (action potential) from one neuron to another along a synapse.
[Line 26] * Synapse: junction between 2 neurons: axon terminal of one neuron (presynaptic neuron) and a second neuron (post synaptic neuron).
[Line 27] 
[Line 28] * types of synaptic transmission:
[Line 29] 1- Electric: neurons are communicated by low-resistance gap-channel pathway ? passage of electric current directly.
[Line 30] N.B.: rare in humans and is common in invertebrates.
[Line 31] 2- Chemical: release of a chemical substance (neurotransmitter).
[Line 32] 
[Line 33] > Functions of Synapses:
[Line 34] a. Facilitating transmission.
[Line 35] b. Blocking transmission.
[Line 36] c. Amplification of impulses.
[Line 37] d. Distribution of impulses.
[Line 38] e. Changing one impulse to repetitive impulses.
[Line 39] f. Integration between different types of neurons.
[Line 40] g. Interpretation, processing and Storage of information.
[Line 41] N.B. synapses are dynamic structures: increasing and decreasing in complexity and number with use and experience.

[Line 43] > Functional Anatomy of a Synapse
[Line 44] 1) Presynaptic Terminal: (or synaptic knob): contains:
[Line 45] a. mitochondria: provides ATP for synthesis and exocytosis of transmitters.
[Line 46] b. A large number of synaptic vesicles contain synaptic transmitters. three types:
[Line 47] 1. Small clear for rapidly acting transmitters e.g. acetyl choline, glycine, glutamate and GABA.
[Line 48] 2. Small granular containing catecholamines.
[Line 49] 3. Large granular contain slowly acting neuropeptides.
[Line 50] (2) synaptic cleft: space containing ECF rich in Na+, Cl- poor in K+.
[Line 51] (3) postsynaptic membrane (PSM): contains Receptors for neurotransmitters. types:
[Line 52] a. Ionotropic receptors: part of Ligand-gated ion channels: affect neuronal activity directly
[Line 53] b. Metabotropric receptors: (mostly G protein coupled receptors) affect activity neuron indirectly by second messenger e.g. cAMP: slower and more prolonged effects, as:
[Line 54] a. Open or close specific ion channels.
[Line 55] b. Activate enzymes e.g. protein kinases: phosphorylation of proteins.
[Line 56] c. Activate gene transcription: formation of new proteins.
[Line 57] Mechanism of Synaptic Transmission
[Line 58] 1- Release of chemical transmitter from presynaptic nerve:
[Line 59] * Arrival of action potential opens voltage-gated Ca++ channels
[Line 60] * Ca++ enters knob according to concentration and electric gradients.
[Line 61] * Influx of Ca++ triggers binding of SNARE proteins:
[Line 62] o V- SNARE (Synaptobrevin) on synaptic vesicle.
[Line 63] o T- SNARE (Syntaxin) on presynaptic membrane
[Line 64] * exocytosis of chemical transmitter into the synaptic cleft.
[Line 65] * N.B.: amount of transmitter is proportional to amount of Ca++ entered.
[Line 66] 2- Binding of Transmitter with its Receptors: changes permeability to ions.
[Line 67] 3- Generation of Postsynaptic Potential: Change in ion fluxes leads to change in its membrane potential ? may become:
[Line 68] a. less negative = excitatory postsynaptic potential. (EPSP).
[Line 69] b. more negative = inhibitory postsynaptic potential. (IPSP).
[Line 70]  magnitude and duration of EPSP and IPSP depends on amount of released Neurotransmitter. "not All or None".
[Line 71] 4- Removal of Neurotransmitters from the Synaptic Cleft:
[Line 72] a. Passive diffusion away from synaptic cleft
[Line 73] b. Inactivation by specific enzymes within synaptic cleft, e.g. acetyl choline
[Line 74] c. Active reuptake into axon terminals, e.g. catecholamines, dopamine, serotonin.
[Line 75] d. Removal by glial cells.

[Line 149] Factors affecting synaptic transmission
[Line 150] 1-  Changes in internal environment:
[Line 151] a. pH:
[Line 152] * Alkalosis: increases neurons excitability & facilitates transmission: decrease ionized Ca++. (may lead to convulsions: if epileptic patient hyperventilates, this may precipitate a fit)
[Line 153] * Acidosis: decreases synaptic transmission due to increase ionized Ca++ (may lead to coma, e.g. in diabetic Ketoacidosis).
[Line 154] b. Hypoxia: inhibits synaptic transmission: interruption of cerebral circulation for 3-5 seconds may cause unconsciousness & prolonged ischemia for minutes cause brain damage.
[Line 155] c. Hypoglycemia: inhibits synaptic transmission (Glucose is the main fuel of brain)
[Line 156] d. Hypocalcemia: facilities transmission as it increases excitability of the post synaptic membrane (leading to tetanic convulsions)

[Line 353] Sensory Receptors
[Line 354] * Definitions:
[Line 355] * Sensory receptor: specialized (modified) structure at peripheral termination of afferent nerve fibers.
[Line 356] * Sensory unit: single sensory afferent with all its receptor endings.
[Line 357] * Receptive field of a neuron: region which contains all the receptor endings of a sensory neuron (stimulus produces a response in that afferent neuron).
[Line 358] 
[Line 359] * Functions:
[Line 360] 1- Detectors: detect stimuli (changes in the internal or external environment).
[Line 361] 2- Transducers: transform energy in stimulus (chemical, mechanical, etc) into electrical energy (receptor potential).
[Line 362] 3- Generators: generation of action potential (nerve impulses) in the sensory nerves
[Line 363] N.B.: Signal Transduction: process by which a stimulus is transformed into an electrical response
[Line 364] 
[Line 365] * Properties of receptors:
[Line 366] [1] Specificity:
[Line 367] * Each type of receptor is highly sensitive to one specific type of stimulus = adequate stimulus "e.g. light, sound, pressure, etc.".
[Line 368] * Receptors respond to other stimuli but:
[Line 369] 1) much higher strength
[Line 370] 2) sensation perceived is that of adequate stimulus.
[Line 371] e.g. rods and cones in retina can respond to a strong mechanical stimulus, but give sensation of light
[Line 372] 
[Line 373] [2] Excitability:
[Line 374] * When stimulated, a receptor generates "Receptor (or Generator) Potential": graded electric response:
[Line 375] (as an example: Paccinian corpuscle was chosen . Pressing on capsule by a rod leads to development of receptor potential).
[Line 376] 
[Line 377] * Transduction of Sensory Signals = (Mechanism of Receptor Potential = Generator Potential) 1- Stimulation of the receptor:
[Line 378] a. stimulus causes opening of ion channels "transduction channels" ? Na+ or Ca++ inflow.
[Line 379] b. number of opened channels is proportional to intensity of the stimulus
[Line 380] 2- Creation of Receptor Potential: Na+ or Ca++ influx creates local partial depolarization
[Line 381] 
[Line 382] 3- Electrotonic spread of receptor potential: local circuit of current (current sink) spreads passively to adjacent part of sensory nerve or 1st node of Ranvier (myelinated neurons) = action potential (A.P.) generating zone (it has low firing level = many voltage gated Na+ channels)
[Line 383] 
[Line 384] 4- Generation of Action Potential: If amplitude of receptor potential is strong to depolarize [A.P. generating zone] to threshold, it opens voltage gated Na+ channels and an action potential is produced & propagated along the sensory nerve to CNS.
[Line 385] 
[Line 386] * Properties of Receptor Potential (local response):
[Line 387] 1. non-propagated
[Line 388] 2. does not obey all or none-law, i.e. can be graded.
[Line 389] 3. no absolute refractory period, i.e. can be summated.
[Line 390] 4. duration (5-10 msec.) is longer than action potential duration (2 msec.) so can cause repeated action potentials.
[Line 391] 5. maximum amplitude is 100 mV (at maximum permeability to Na+ ions).
[Line 392] 
[Line 393] * Relationship between strength of stimulus & magnitude	of receptor potential: As strength of stimulus increases, magnitude of the R.P. increases.
[Line 394] * N.B.:
[Line 395] o amplitude increases rapidly at first then less rapidly at high stimulus strengths.
[Line 396] o allows receptor to be sensitive (have an extreme range of response) to very weak sensory stimuli without reaching maximum firing until sensory experience is extreme
[Line 397] * Relation of the receptor potential magnitude to number of impulses generated:
[Line 398] o The more the receptor potential rises above threshold level, the greater the frequency of the action potential
[Line 399] 
[Line 400] [3] Adaptation of receptors:
[Line 401] * Definition: gradual decline of receptor potential and frequency of action potentials generated in spite of the continued presence of stimulus at constant strength.
[Line 402] * Causes: Each receptor has its own property; adaptation of mechanoreceptors occurs by:
[Line 403] a. Readjustment and remolding in the structure.
[Line 404] b. Accommodation of terminal nerve fiber or 1st node of Ranvier to the stimulus, due to inactivation of Na+ channels.
[Line 405] * receptors are classified into:
[Line 406] Rapidly adapting (phasic)
[Line 407] Slowly adapting (Tonic)
[Line 408] adapt rapidly
[Line 409] either slowly adapting or they do not adapt at all
[Line 410] * discharge strongly when a change is taking place
[Line 411] * response when the stimulus is "ON" and another response
[Line 412] when the stimulus is "OFF"
[Line 413] discharge as long as the stimulus is applied
[Line 414] detect movement, rate of movement and vibration e.g. touch
[Line 415] and pressure receptors (Meissner's & Paccinian corpuscles).
[Line 416] * Keep the brain continuously informed e.g. pain receptors,
[Line 417] muscle spindlesand arterial baroreceptors.

[Line 421] Sensory Code
[Line 422] Definition: ability of CNS to recognize modality (type), locality and intensity of sensation. (All stimuli produce action potentials)
[Line 423] A) Modality of sensation: depends on:
[Line 424] 1. Adequate Stimulus: Each receptor is highly sensitive to a particular type of stimulus.
[Line 425] 2. Muller's law of specific nerve energy: Whatever method of stimulation, sensation given is that the receptor is specialized to.
[Line 426]    3. Labeled line principle: Each sensation reaches CNS in a specific pathway: Stimulation of this pathway at any point by any form evokes its specific sensation.
[Line 427] B) Locality of the stimulus:
[Line 428] * Law of Projection: There is a separate representation area for each part of the body in the cerebral cortex, (i.e. point to point representation). When an impulse reaches the specific area in the cortex, it "projects" this stimulus to its original site.
[Line 429] * Phantom limb: a phenomenon in which pressure on the nerves in the stump of an amputated limb makes the patient feel the pain as though it is coming from the absent limb
[Line 430] C) Intensity of sensation: It is encoded by change in:
[Line 431] 1- Number of receptors activated: Higher intensity stimuli will recruit more receptors ["recruitment" of receptors]
[Line 432] 2- Frequency of impulses: Increasing stimulus intensity increase frequency of impulses with increased intensity of sensation felt.
