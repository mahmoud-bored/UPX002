[Line 16] Synaptic Transmission
[Line 17] * Definition: transmission of impulse (action potential) from one neuron to another along a synapse.
[Line 18] * Synapse: junction between 2 neurons: axon terminal of one neuron (presynaptic neuron) and a second neuron (post synaptic neuron).
[Line 19] 
[Line 20] * types of synaptic transmission:
[Line 21] 1- Electric: neurons are communicated by low-resistance gap-channel pathway ? passage of electric current directly.
[Line 22] N.B.: rare in humans and is common in invertebrates.
[Line 23] 2- Chemical: release of a chemical substance (neurotransmitter).

[Line 26] a. Facilitating transmission.
[Line 27] b. Blocking transmission.
[Line 28] c. Amplification of impulses.
[Line 29] d. Distribution of impulses.
[Line 30] e. Changing one impulse to repetitive impulses.
[Line 31] f. Integration between different types of neurons.
[Line 32] g. Interpretation, processing and Storage of information.
[Line 33] N.B. synapses are dynamic structures: increasing and decreasing in complexity and number with use and experience.

[Line 35] > Functional Anatomy of a Synapse
[Line 36] 1) Presynaptic Terminal: (or synaptic knob): contains:
[Line 37] a. mitochondria: provides ATP for synthesis and exocytosis of transmitters.
[Line 38] b. A large number of synaptic vesicles contain synaptic transmitters. three types:
[Line 39] 1. Small clear for rapidly acting transmitters e.g. acetyl choline, glycine, glutamate and GABA.
[Line 40] 2. Small granular containing catecholamines.
[Line 41] 3. Large granular contain slowly acting neuropeptides.
[Line 42] (2) synaptic cleft: space containing ECF rich in Na+, Cl- poor in K+.
[Line 43] (3) postsynaptic membrane (PSM): contains Receptors for neurotransmitters. types:
[Line 44] a. Ionotropic receptors: part of Ligand-gated ion channels: affect neuronal activity directly
[Line 45] b. Metabotropric receptors: (mostly G protein coupled receptors) affect activity neuron indirectly by second messenger e.g. cAMP: slower and more prolonged effects, as:
[Line 46] a. Open or close specific ion channels.
[Line 47] b. Activate enzymes e.g. protein kinases: phosphorylation of proteins.
[Line 48] c. Activate gene transcription: formation of new proteins.
[Line 49] Mechanism of Synaptic Transmission
[Line 50] 1- Release of chemical transmitter from presynaptic nerve:
[Line 51] * Arrival of action potential opens voltage-gated Ca++ channels
[Line 52] * Ca++ enters knob according to concentration and electric gradients.
[Line 53] * Influx of Ca++ triggers binding of SNARE proteins:
[Line 54] o V- SNARE (Synaptobrevin) on synaptic vesicle.
[Line 55] o T- SNARE (Syntaxin) on presynaptic membrane
[Line 56] * exocytosis of chemical transmitter into the synaptic cleft.
[Line 57] * N.B.: amount of transmitter is proportional to amount of Ca++ entered.
[Line 58] 2- Binding of Transmitter with its Receptors: changes permeability to ions.
[Line 59] 3- Generation of Postsynaptic Potential: Change in ion fluxes leads to change in its membrane potential ? may become:
[Line 60] a. less negative = excitatory postsynaptic potential. (EPSP).
[Line 61] b. more negative = inhibitory postsynaptic potential. (IPSP).
[Line 62]  magnitude and duration of EPSP and IPSP depends on amount of released Neurotransmitter. "not All or None".
[Line 63] 4- Removal of Neurotransmitters from the Synaptic Cleft:
[Line 64] a. Passive diffusion away from synaptic cleft
[Line 65] b. Inactivation by specific enzymes within synaptic cleft, e.g. acetyl choline
[Line 66] c. Active reuptake into axon terminals, e.g. catecholamines, dopamine, serotonin.
[Line 67] d. Removal by glial cells.

[Line 141] Factors affecting synaptic transmission
[Line 142] 1-  Changes in internal environment:
[Line 143] a. pH:
[Line 144] * Alkalosis: increases neurons excitability & facilitates transmission: decrease ionized Ca++. (may lead to convulsions: if epileptic patient hyperventilates, this may precipitate a fit)
[Line 145] * Acidosis: decreases synaptic transmission due to increase ionized Ca++ (may lead to coma, e.g. in diabetic Ketoacidosis).

[Line 345] Sensory Receptors
[Line 346] * Definitions:
[Line 347] * Sensory receptor: specialized (modified) structure at peripheral termination of afferent nerve fibers.
[Line 348] * Sensory unit: single sensory afferent with all its receptor endings.
[Line 349] * Receptive field of a neuron: region which contains all the receptor endings of a sensory neuron (stimulus produces a response in that afferent neuron).
[Line 350] 
[Line 351] * Functions:
[Line 352] 1- Detectors: detect stimuli (changes in the internal or external environment).
[Line 353] 2- Transducers: transform energy in stimulus (chemical, mechanical, etc) into electrical energy (receptor potential).
[Line 354] 3- Generators: generation of action potential (nerve impulses) in the sensory nerves
[Line 355] N.B.: Signal Transduction: process by which a stimulus is transformed into an electrical response
[Line 356] 
[Line 357] * Properties of receptors:
[Line 358] [1] Specificity:
[Line 359] * Each type of receptor is highly sensitive to one specific type of stimulus = adequate stimulus "e.g. light, sound, pressure, etc.".
[Line 360] * Receptors respond to other stimuli but:
[Line 361] 1) much higher strength
[Line 362] 2) sensation perceived is that of adequate stimulus.
[Line 363] e.g. rods and cones in retina can respond to a strong mechanical stimulus, but give sensation of light
[Line 364] 
[Line 365] [2] Excitability:
[Line 366] * When stimulated, a receptor generates "Receptor (or Generator) Potential": graded electric response:
[Line 367] (as an example: Paccinian corpuscle was chosen . Pressing on capsule by a rod leads to development of receptor potential).
[Line 368] 
[Line 369] * Transduction of Sensory Signals = (Mechanism of Receptor Potential = Generator Potential) 1- Stimulation of the receptor:
[Line 370] a. stimulus causes opening of ion channels "transduction channels" ? Na+ or Ca++ inflow.
[Line 371] b. number of opened channels is proportional to intensity of the stimulus
[Line 372] 2- Creation of Receptor Potential: Na+ or Ca++ influx creates local partial depolarization
[Line 373] 
[Line 374] 3- Electrotonic spread of receptor potential: local circuit of current (current sink) spreads passively to adjacent part of sensory nerve or 1st node of Ranvier (myelinated neurons) = action potential (A.P.) generating zone (it has low firing level = many voltage gated Na+ channels)
[Line 375] 
[Line 376] 4- Generation of Action Potential: If amplitude of receptor potential is strong to depolarize [A.P. generating zone] to threshold, it opens voltage gated Na+ channels and an action potential is produced & propagated along the sensory nerve to CNS.
[Line 377] 
[Line 378] * Properties of Receptor Potential (local response):
[Line 379] 1. non-propagated
[Line 380] 2. does not obey all or none-law, i.e. can be graded.
[Line 381] 3. no absolute refractory period, i.e. can be summated.
[Line 382] 4. duration (5-10 msec.) is longer than action potential duration (2 msec.) so can cause repeated action potentials.
[Line 383] 5. maximum amplitude is 100 mV (at maximum permeability to Na+ ions).
[Line 384] 
[Line 385] * Relationship between strength of stimulus & magnitude	of receptor potential: As strength of stimulus increases, magnitude of the R.P. increases.
[Line 386] * N.B.:
[Line 387] o amplitude increases rapidly at first then less rapidly at high stimulus strengths.
[Line 388] o allows receptor to be sensitive (have an extreme range of response) to very weak sensory stimuli without reaching maximum firing until sensory experience is extreme
[Line 389] * Relation of the receptor potential magnitude to number of impulses generated:
[Line 390] o The more the receptor potential rises above threshold level, the greater the frequency of the action potential
[Line 391] 
[Line 392] [3] Adaptation of receptors:
[Line 393] * Definition: gradual decline of receptor potential and frequency of action potentials generated in spite of the continued presence of stimulus at constant strength.
[Line 394] * Causes: Each receptor has its own property; adaptation of mechanoreceptors occurs by:
[Line 395] a. Readjustment and remolding in the structure.
[Line 396] b. Accommodation of terminal nerve fiber or 1st node of Ranvier to the stimulus, due to inactivation of Na+ channels.
[Line 397] * receptors are classified into:
[Line 398] Rapidly adapting (phasic)
[Line 399] Slowly adapting (Tonic)
[Line 400] adapt rapidly
[Line 401] either slowly adapting or they do not adapt at all
[Line 402] * discharge strongly when a change is taking place
[Line 403] * response when the stimulus is "ON" and another response
[Line 404] when the stimulus is "OFF"
[Line 405] discharge as long as the stimulus is applied
[Line 406] detect movement, rate of movement and vibration e.g. touch

[Line 413] Sensory Code
[Line 414] Definition: ability of CNS to recognize modality (type), locality and intensity of sensation. (All stimuli produce action potentials)
[Line 415] A) Modality of sensation: depends on:
[Line 416] 1. Adequate Stimulus: Each receptor is highly sensitive to a particular type of stimulus.
[Line 417] 2. Muller's law of specific nerve energy: Whatever method of stimulation, sensation given is that the receptor is specialized to.
[Line 418]    3. Labeled line principle: Each sensation reaches CNS in a specific pathway: Stimulation of this pathway at any point by any form evokes its specific sensation.
[Line 419] B) Locality of the stimulus:
[Line 420] * Law of Projection: There is a separate representation area for each part of the body in the cerebral cortex, (i.e. point to point representation). When an impulse reaches the specific area in the cortex, it "projects" this stimulus to its original site.
[Line 421] * Phantom limb: a phenomenon in which pressure on the nerves in the stump of an amputated limb makes the patient feel the pain as though it is coming from the absent limb
[Line 422] C) Intensity of sensation: It is encoded by change in:
[Line 423] 1- Number of receptors activated: Higher intensity stimuli will recruit more receptors ["recruitment" of receptors]
[Line 424] 2- Frequency of impulses: Increasing stimulus intensity increase frequency of impulses with increased intensity of sensation felt.
