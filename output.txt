[Line 22]:                                         Synaptic Transmission
[Line 23]: •   Definition: transmission of impulse (action potential) from one neuron to another
[Line 24]:     along a synapse.

[Line 47]:          1.  Small clear      for  rapidly acting transmitters e.g. acetyl choline, glycine,

[Line 51]:  (2) synaptic cleft: space containing ECF rich in Na+, Cl- poor in K+.

[Line 53]:     a.  Ionotropic receptors:         part of   Ligand-gated       ion channels:       affect neuronal
[Line 54]:         activity directly
[Line 55]:     b. Metabotropric receptors: (mostly G protein coupled receptors) affect activity
[Line 56]:         neuron    indirectly     by   second  messenger         e.g.   cAMP:      slower     and   more

[Line 58]:             a. Open or close specific ion channels.

[Line 61]:                                Mechanism of Synaptic Transmission
[Line 62]: 1-   Release of chemical transmitter from presynaptic nerve:
[Line 63]:     •   Arrival of action potential opens voltage-gated Ca++ channels
[Line 64]:     •   Ca++ enters knob according to concentration and electric gradients.
[Line 65]:     •   Influx of Ca++ triggers binding of SNARE proteins:
[Line 66]:             o   V- SNARE (Synaptobrevin) on synaptic vesicle.
[Line 67]:             o    T- SNARE (Syntaxin) on presynaptic membrane
[Line 68]:     •   exocytosis of chemical transmitter into the synaptic cleft.
[Line 69]:     •   N.B.: amount of transmitter is proportional to amount of Ca++ entered.
[Line 70]: 2- Binding of Transmitter with its Receptors: changes permeability to ions.
[Line 71]: 3- Generation of Postsynaptic Potential: Change in ion fluxes leads to change in its
[Line 72]: membrane potential → may become:
[Line 73]:          a.  less negative = excitatory postsynaptic potential. (EPSP).
[Line 74]:          b.  more negative = inhibitory postsynaptic potential. (IPSP).
[Line 75]:        magnitude  and  duration       of  EPSP  and  IPSP  depends  on  amount  of  released
[Line 76]:       Neurotransmitter. “not All or None”.
[Line 77]: 4- Removal of Neurotransmitters from the Synaptic Cleft:
[Line 78]:       a.  Passive diffusion away from synaptic cleft
[Line 79]:       b.  Inactivation by specific enzymes within synaptic cleft, e.g. acetyl choline
[Line 80]:       c.  Active reuptake into axon terminals, e.g. catecholamines, dopamine, serotonin.
[Line 81]:       d.  Removal by glial cells.

[Line 128]:                                                            Factors affecting synaptic transmission
[Line 129]:    1- Changes in internal environment:
[Line 130]:      a.  pH:
[Line 131]:         •   Alkalosis: increases neurons excitability & facilitates transmission: decrease ionized Ca++. (may lead to convulsions: if
[Line 132]:             epileptic patient hyperventilates, this may precipitate a fit)
[Line 133]:         •   Acidosis: decreases synaptic transmission due to increase ionized Ca++ (may lead to coma, e.g. in diabetic Ketoacidosis).
[Line 134]:      b.  Hypoxia: inhibits synaptic transmission: interruption of cerebral circulation for 3-5 seconds may cause unconsciousness &
[Line 135]:          prolonged ischemia for minutes cause brain damage.
[Line 136]:      c.  Hypoglycemia: inhibits synaptic transmission (Glucose is the main fuel of brain)
[Line 137]:      d.  Hypocalcemia: facilities transmission as it increases excitability of the post synaptic membrane (leading to tetanic convulsions)
[Line 138]:   2- Drugs: Most of the drugs act on the CNS alter synaptic mechanism, e.g.:
[Line 139]:      a. Theophylline, theobromine, and caffeine ↑ transmission by depolarizing the postsynaptic membrane.
[Line 140]:      b. Analgesics, hypnotics and anaesthetics: ↓ transmission = stabilizing membrane causing hyperpolarization or by interference
[Line 141]:         with synthesis, release or reuptake of certain neurotransmitters.
[Line 142]:      c. Strychnine: ↑↑↑↑ transmission: competes with inhibitory transmitters leaving excitatory pathways unaffected: convulsions.
[Line 143]:     3- Diseases:
[Line 144]:                                     Tetanus toxin                                                                  Botulism toxin

[Line 260]:                                                                         Sensory Receptors
[Line 261]: ❖   Definitions:
[Line 262]:     •   Sensory receptor: specialized (modified) structure at peripheral termination of afferent nerve fibers.
[Line 263]:     •   Sensory unit: single sensory afferent with all its receptor endings.
[Line 264]:     •   Receptive field of a neuron: region which contains all the receptor endings of a sensory neuron (stimulus produces a response
[Line 265]:         in that afferent neuron).
[Line 266]: ❖   Functions:
[Line 267]:     1- Detectors: detect stimuli (changes in the internal or external environment).
[Line 268]:     2- Transducers: transform energy in stimulus (chemical, mechanical, etc) into electrical energy (receptor potential).
[Line 269]:     3- Generators: generation of action potential (nerve impulses) in the sensory nerves
[Line 270]: N.B.: Signal Transduction: process by which a stimulus is transformed into an electrical response
[Line 271]: ❖   Properties of receptors:
[Line 272]: [1] Specificity:
[Line 273]: •   Each type of receptor is highly sensitive to one specific type of stimulus = adequate stimulus “e.g. light, sound, pressure, etc.”.
[Line 274]: •   Receptors respond to other stimuli but:
[Line 275]:     1)   much higher strength
[Line 276]:     2)  sensation perceived is that of adequate stimulus.
[Line 277]:     e.g. rods and cones in retina can respond to a strong mechanical stimulus, but give sensation of light
[Line 278]:                                    A.F. 2023                                                                                        12
[Page 13]
[Line 279]: [2] Excitability:
[Line 280]: •   When stimulated, a receptor generates "Receptor (or Generator) Potential":  graded electric response:
[Line 281]:     (as an example: Paccinian corpuscle was chosen . Pressing on capsule by a rod leads to development of receptor potential).
[Line 282]: ❖   Transduction of Sensory Signals = (Mechanism of Receptor Potential = Generator Potential)
[Line 283]: 1-  Stimulation of the receptor:
[Line 284]:       a. stimulus causes opening of ion channels “transduction channels” → Na+ or Ca++ inflow.
[Line 285]:       b. number of opened channels is proportional to intensity of the stimulus
[Line 286]: 2-  Creation of Receptor Potential: Na+ or Ca++ influx creates local partial depolarization
[Line 287]: 3-  Electrotonic spread of receptor potential: local circuit of current (current sink) spreads passively to adjacent part of sensory
[Line 288]:     nerve or 1st node of Ranvier (myelinated neurons) = action potential (A.P.) generating zone (it has low firing level = many voltage
[Line 289]:     gated Na+ channels)
[Line 290]: 4- Generation of Action Potential: If amplitude of receptor potential is strong to depolarize [A.P. generating zone] to threshold, it
[Line 291]:       opens voltage gated Na+ channels and an action potential is produced & propagated along the sensory nerve to CNS.
[Line 292]: ❖   Properties of Receptor Potential (local response):
[Line 293]:     1.  non-propagated
[Line 294]:     2.  does not obey all or none-law, i.e. can be graded.
[Line 295]:     3.  no absolute refractory period, i.e. can be summated.
[Line 296]:     4.  duration (5-10 msec.) is longer than action potential duration (2 msec.) so can cause repeated action potentials.
[Line 297]:     5.  maximum amplitude is 100 mV (at maximum permeability to Na+ ions).

[Line 323]:                                                                          Sensory Code
[Line 324]: Definition: ability of CNS to recognize modality (type), locality and intensity of sensation. (All stimuli produce action potentials)
[Line 325]: A) Modality of sensation: depends on:
[Line 326]:     1.  Adequate Stimulus: Each receptor is highly sensitive to a particular type of stimulus.
[Line 327]:     2.  Muller’s law of specific nerve energy: Whatever method of stimulation, sensation given is that the receptor is specialized to.
[Line 328]:     3. Labeled line principle: Each sensation reaches CNS in a specific pathway: Stimulation of this pathway at any point by any
[Line 329]: form evokes its specific sensation.
[Line 330]: B) Locality of the stimulus:
[Line 331]:     •   Law of Projection: There is a separate representation area for each part of the body in the cerebral cortex, (i.e. point to point
[Line 332]:         representation). When an impulse reaches the specific area in the cortex, it "projects" this stimulus to its original site.
[Line 333]:     •   Phantom limb: a phenomenon in which pressure on the nerves in the stump of an amputated limb makes the patient feel the
[Line 334]:         pain as though it is coming from the absent limb
[Line 335]: C) Intensity of sensation: It is encoded by change in:
[Line 336]:     1-  Number of receptors activated: Higher intensity stimuli will recruit more receptors ["recruitment” of receptors]
[Line 337]:     2-  Frequency of impulses: Increasing stimulus intensity increase frequency of impulses with increased intensity of sensation felt.

[Line 465]:                                                                         III- Pain Sensation
[Line 466]: •   Pain: unpleasant protective sensory and emotional experience if there is physical or potential tissue damage.
[Line 467]: ➢   Pain Receptors (Nociceptors): Free Nerve Endings
[Line 468]: ❖   types:
[Line 469]:     1-   Mechanical: stimulated by mechanical injurious stimuli, e.g. cuts, bruises.
[Line 470]:     2-   Thermal: respond to extremes of temperature and capsaicin (substance present in hot chilli).
[Line 471]:     3-   Chemical: stimulated by chemical injurious elements.
[Line 472]:     4-   Polymodal: respond to all stimuli.
[Line 473]: ❖   Distribution of Pain receptors:
[Line 474]: •   most numerous in superficial layers of the skin, periosteum, peritoneum, pleura, joints, arterial walls, dura and tentorium.
[Line 475]: •   less distributed in deep tissues and very few in internal viscera.
[Line 476]: •   absent in liver parenchyma, lung alveoli, and brain tissue (pain insensitive structures).
